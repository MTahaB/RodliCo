\title{\textbf{RoDiLoCo}: How Fragile is Low-Communication Training,\\ and Can Classical Byzantine Defenses Save It?}
\author{Taha Benkiran}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Low-communication training methods such as DiLoCo~\citep{diloco2023} promise
\emph{cross-organisation}, \emph{over-the-internet} pretraining, exchanging pseudo-gradients
only every $H$ inner steps and matching synchronous quality at two orders of magnitude less
communication. Yet this literature assumes \emph{honest} workers---precisely the assumption
that the cross-organisation setting violates. We ask three questions. (i)~How fragile is
vanilla DiLoCo: what Byzantine fraction $f$ suffices to derail a run, and does the
synchronisation period $H$ modulate that fragility? (ii)~Do classical Byzantine-robust
aggregators (Krum, coordinate-wise trimmed mean, geometric median) \emph{transfer} to
DiLoCo's outer regime---aggregating pseudo-gradients accumulated over $H$ AdamW steps and
consumed by outer Nesterov momentum, at only $n\!\approx\!8$ workers? (iii)~What is the
\emph{robustness tax}: the quality lost, absent any attack, from using a robust aggregator?
We further propose a trust-weighted outer optimizer that scores workers by the temporal
consistency of their pseudo-gradients and \emph{weights} rather than \emph{excludes} them.
On a small char-level testbed with sequentially-simulated workers, we find that a single
byzantine worker of eight diverges vanilla DiLoCo; that classical defenses transfer partially
(Krum, geometric median and centered clipping hold, while coordinate-wise trimmed mean fails
once $f$ exceeds its trim); and that the proposed trust-weighted optimizer is simultaneously
the cheapest and the most robust defense in the study. Code:
\url{https://github.com/MTahaB/RoDiLoCo}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
DiLoCo and its descendants~\citep{diloco2023,streamingdiloco2025,dilocoscaling2025} make a
striking promise: independent parties, poorly connected, can jointly pretrain a single model
while communicating hundreds of times less than synchronous data-parallel training. The
motivating vision is explicitly \emph{cross-organisation} and \emph{over-the-internet}. But
that vision is in tension with a silent premise of the method: every worker is assumed to
report its pseudo-gradient honestly. A single corrupted worker at the outer aggregation
step---where per-worker pseudo-gradients are averaged---can, in principle, destroy an entire
pretraining run.

This gap is not addressed by existing resilience work. Decoupled
DiLoCo~\citep{decoupleddiloco2026} hardens DiLoCo against \emph{hardware failures}: workers
that go absent. That is \emph{fault-tolerance} (crash-stop, honest-but-absent), not
\emph{Byzantine-tolerance} (present-and-adversarial). The two are different problems, and the
latter is, to our knowledge, unstudied for DiLoCo.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A \textbf{fragility characterization} of vanilla DiLoCo under three attack families,
  quantifying the Byzantine fraction that derails training and the (non-obvious) effect of
  the synchronisation period $H$ (\S\ref{sec:fragility}).
  \item A \textbf{transfer study}: whether Krum, trimmed mean, and geometric median---designed
  for the inner-gradient, large-$n$ federated regime---remain effective over DiLoCo's
  outer-momentum, pseudo-gradient, small-$n$ regime, together with the \textbf{robustness
  tax} they levy at $f=0$ (\S\ref{sec:defenses}).
  \item A \textbf{trust-weighted outer optimizer} that exploits the temporal structure unique
  to the outer setting, weighting workers rather than excluding them (\S\ref{sec:defenses}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{DiLoCo.}
DiLoCo alternates $H$ inner AdamW~\citep{adamw2019} steps on each worker's data shard with a
single outer step. Worker $k$ produces a pseudo-gradient
$\dg_k = \theta_{\text{before}} - \theta_{\text{after}}$; the server aggregates
$\agg = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_k \dg_k$ and applies an outer Nesterov step. It is a variant of
FedAvg~\citep{fedavg2017} with a large number of inner steps and a momentum-based outer
optimizer~\citep{outeroptimizers2025}.

\paragraph{Byzantine-robust aggregation.}
The federated-learning literature offers robust replacements for the mean: Krum, which selects
the update closest to its nearest neighbours~\citep{krum2017}; coordinate-wise trimmed
mean~\citep{trimmedmean2018}; the geometric median; and centered
clipping~\citep{centeredclipping2021}, which clips updates around a running center and is
notable here because that center behaves like server momentum---a natural, rather than
awkward, fit for DiLoCo's outer-Nesterov step. These were designed for raw stochastic
\emph{gradients} at \emph{large} $n$. Recent work even shows the mean can outdo robust
aggregators under some heterogeneous poisoning~\citep{meanrobust2024}, underscoring that
transfer to a new regime is not a given. Robustness is further stress-tested by
\emph{omniscient} attacks that read the honest updates and craft a colluding vector inside
the honest cloud~\citep{alie2019,minmax2021}, which evade distance- and clustering-based
defenses by construction.

\paragraph{The gap.}
DiLoCo's outer step differs from that setting on three axes at once: it aggregates
\emph{pseudo-gradients} (accumulated over $H$ AdamW steps, not single gradients), those are
consumed by \emph{outer momentum}, and $n$ is \emph{small} ($\approx 8$). The nearest
adjacent work, DeMoA~\citep{demoa2025}, combines Byzantine robustness, communication
efficiency, and momentum---but in federated learning over client gradients, not DiLoCo
pretraining. RoDiLoCo studies the empty intersection.

\section{Threat Model and Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Model and data.}
We train a decoder-only Transformer (RMSNorm, RoPE, SwiGLU) of $1.8$M parameters
($d_{\text{model}}{=}192$, $4$ layers, $6$ heads) on a byte-level TinyStories
subset~\citep{tinystories2023}, at nanochat scale~\citep{nanochatdiloco2025}. Hyperparameters
are in Table~\ref{tab:hp}.

\paragraph{Simulated workers.}
Following the resource constraints of a single-GPU study, the $n$ workers are simulated
\emph{sequentially} on one device: for each worker we load the global parameters, run $H$
inner steps on its shard, and store $\dg_k$; we then aggregate and take the outer step.
Communication is accounted \emph{analytically} (bytes that would be exchanged), never
physically transmitted. Data is partitioned i.i.d.\ across workers (non-i.i.d.\ is an
ablation).

\paragraph{Threat model.}
Up to $f$ of $n$ workers are Byzantine and may (a) report a falsified $\dg_k$, or (b) train
honestly on a corrupted objective. We consider two families. \textbf{Blind} attacks tamper
only with the adversary's own update: \emph{sign-flip} ($\dg_k \to -\lambda\dg_k$),
\emph{scaled noise} ($\dg_k$ replaced by noise calibrated to the honest norm), and
\emph{targeted drift} (inner poisoning via label permutation, which keeps $\|\dg_k\|$
plausible). \textbf{Omniscient} attacks let the colluding adversaries observe the honest
updates and emit a single crafted vector that maximally biases the aggregate while remaining
inside the honest cloud: \emph{ALIE}~\citep{alie2019} shifts each coordinate a fraction $z$
of a standard deviation below the honest mean; the \emph{min-max} agnostic
attack~\citep{minmax2021} scales a deviation direction to the largest magnitude that keeps the
malicious point within the honest cloud's diameter. The server knows neither which workers are
Byzantine nor $f$ exactly.

\paragraph{Defenses.}
All defenses share one interface, $\texttt{aggregate}(\{\dg_k\}) \to \agg$: mean (baseline),
trimmed mean, Krum/Multi-Krum, geometric median, centered clipping, and our trust-weighted
optimizer (\S\ref{sec:defenses}).

\section{Fragility of Vanilla DiLoCo}
\label{sec:fragility}
With mean aggregation and no attack, DiLoCo trains cleanly (final perplexity $3.37$) while
communicating $\sim H\times$ less than synchronous data-parallel. We then sweep the Byzantine
fraction $f \in \{0,1,2,3\}/8$ under sign-flip. The mean is catastrophically fragile: healthy
at $f{=}0$, it diverges (perplexity hits our $e^{20}$ cap) at $f{=}1$ and beyond
(Figure~\ref{fig:fragility}).

\paragraph{Finding 1 (fragility).} Under sign-flip, $f{=}1/8$ is already sufficient to diverge
vanilla DiLoCo: final perplexity goes from $3.37$ ($f{=}0$) to fully diverged ($f\ge 1$). The
promise of cross-organisation training does not survive a single dishonest participant under
the default aggregation.

\section{Defenses: Transfer, Tax, and Trust Weighting}
\label{sec:defenses}

\subsection{Classical and modern robust aggregators}
We port trimmed mean, Krum/Multi-Krum, the geometric median (Weiszfeld), and centered
clipping~\citep{centeredclipping2021} into the outer step and evaluate them against the worst
attack---including the omniscient min-max attack, which is built to evade the distance-based
defenses. Two effects are measured explicitly: the interaction between the defense and outer
momentum (centered clipping's running center coincides with that momentum, which we expect to
help), and the small-$n$ behaviour of Krum (at $n=8$, discarding $f{+}2$ candidates is
information-expensive, and adaptive attacks exploit exactly this).

\subsection{Trust-weighted outer optimizer}
We score each worker by an EMA of the cosine similarity between its pseudo-gradient and the
previous robust aggregate,
\begin{equation}
  s_k(t) = \beta\, s_k(t{-}1) + (1-\beta)\,\cos\!\big(\dg_k(t), \agg(t{-}1)\big),
\end{equation}
and aggregate with weights $\mathrm{softmax}(s/\tau)$. Unlike Krum it weights rather than
excludes, retaining information, and it exploits the temporal dimension that the outer setting
(few rounds, rich $\dg$) makes informative. By design, at $f=0$ all workers stay consistent,
weights approach uniform, and the method reduces to the mean---the ``zero tax'' property we
confirm empirically ($3.30$ at $f{=}0$, the lowest of all aggregators, Table~\ref{tab:results}).
Two design choices proved decisive under strong attacks, each traceable to an observed failure:
(i) a \emph{robust cold-start} (seed the trust reference from a geometric median rather than
the round-0 mean, which is already poisoned) --- this alone moved $f{=}2$ from divergence to
survival; and (ii) a \emph{median-norm rescale} (rescale the aggregate to the median rather
than the mean $\Delta$ norm), preventing a norm-inflating attacker from blowing up the
aggregate magnitude round over round into a NaN.

\paragraph{Finding 3 (transfer and tax).} Classical robust aggregators transfer to the outer
regime, but not uniformly. Krum, geometric median and centered clipping remain robust through
$f{=}2$; centered clipping is the cheapest of these, consistent with its running center
coinciding with the outer momentum. Two clean failure modes appear: coordinate-wise trimmed
mean diverges at $f{=}2$ (with $\text{trim}{=}1$ it cannot remove two byzantine values per
coordinate), and Krum pays the largest robustness tax ($3.99$ vs $3.37$, $\approx\!18\%$) --
the small-$n$ cost of discarding $f{+}2$ candidates. The proposed trust-weighted optimizer is
the only aggregator that is simultaneously the cheapest at $f{=}0$ and the flattest under
attack (perplexity $3.27$--$3.33$ across $f\in\{0,1,2\}$).

\section{Limitations and Directions}
\label{sec:limitations}
Our study is deliberately small-scale: a $1.8$M-parameter model, workers \emph{simulated}
rather than physically distributed, i.i.d.\ shards, a single seed for the headline table, and
one attack family (sign-flip) in the main comparison. The $f{=}2$ result for trust-weighted
being marginally below $f{=}0$ is within seed noise; the honest claim is ``flat'', not
``improves under attack''. Scaling laws for DiLoCo~\citep{dilocoscaling2025} suggest which
conclusions should hold at larger scale, but we do not verify this directly. Natural
extensions: seed replication (mean$\pm$std), the omniscient min-max attack~\citep{minmax2021}
as the main stressor, the asynchronous / Decoupled setting~\citep{decoupleddiloco2026},
non-i.i.d.\ data, and theoretical convergence guarantees under attack.

\section{Conclusion}
Low-communication training is most valuable exactly where its honesty assumption is least
tenable. We quantified DiLoCo's fragility to Byzantine workers (a single worker of eight
suffices to diverge it), measured how far classical defenses transfer and at what cost, and
proposed a trust-weighted outer optimizer that, in our setting, is both the cheapest and the
most robust defense --- turning DiLoCo from resilient-to-faults into robust-to-adversaries at
no cost when no one cheats.
